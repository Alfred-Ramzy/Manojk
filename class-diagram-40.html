Questions 1–8 — Association Relationships
Q1 — Association (One-to-Many)
A Customer can place many Orders. Each Order belongs to exactly one Customer. Orders have an orderID, orderDate, and totalAmount.
TRICK: Association = simple relationship. Arrow → from the class that "uses" or "knows about" the other. Multiplicity: 1 on Customer side, * on Order side.
Q2 — Association (Many-to-Many)
A Student can enroll in many Courses. A Course can have many Students enrolled. This is a many-to-many relationship requiring an association class Enrollment.
TRICK: Many-to-many (*..*) → always needs an association class in the middle to store extra data (enrollmentDate, grade, etc.)
Q3 — Association (One-to-One)
Each Employee is assigned exactly one WorkStation. A WorkStation is assigned to exactly one Employee. Neither can exist in this relationship without the other.
TRICK: One-to-One (1..1 — 1..1) is rare. Both sides have multiplicity 1. Arrow is bidirectional or shown as plain line. Different from composition because both exist independently.
Q4 — Association with Navigability
A Doctor treats many Patients. The Doctor class needs to know about patients (navigable). But a Patient does NOT directly access Doctor information — they go through Appointments.
TRICK: Navigability = arrowhead shows direction of knowledge. Arrow on one side only = one-way navigation. No arrowhead = both can access each other (bidirectional).
Q5 — Association (Zero or more)
A Librarian manages zero or more Books. A Book is managed by exactly one Librarian. Multiplicity on Book side is 0..* (a newly added librarian may not yet manage any books).
TRICK: 0..* means zero to many — the relationship is optional on that side. Compare with 1..* (at least one is required). Always write multiplicity near the class it describes.
Q6 — Association (Reflexive / Self-association)
An Employee can manage other Employees. Each Employee has one manager (another Employee) and can manage zero or more subordinate Employees. This is a self-referencing association.
TRICK: Reflexive association = a class associated with itself. Draw an arrow from the class back to itself. Label both ends differently (manager / subordinate). Common in organizational hierarchy models.
Q7 — Association (with role names)
A Bank Account is associated with a Person. The Person plays the role of "accountHolder" on one side. The Account plays the role of "ownedAccount" on the other side. One person can own multiple accounts.
TRICK: Role names go NEAR the class they describe, on the association line. They clarify the role each class plays in the relationship — different from multiplicity which is a number.
Q8 — Association (Ternary)
A Teacher teaches a Course in a specific Classroom. This three-way relationship cannot be split into two binary associations without losing meaning — it is a ternary association.
TRICK: Ternary association = 3 classes connected to a diamond shape in the middle. Rare but used when the relationship only makes sense with all 3 parties together.
Questions 9–16 — Aggregation & Composition
Q9 — Aggregation (Weak "Has-a")
A Team has many Players. If the Team is dissolved, the Players still exist — they can join other teams. The Player is independent of the Team's lifecycle.
TRICK: Aggregation = hollow diamond ◇ on the OWNER side. Parts survive independently if owner is destroyed. Ask: "Can the part exist without the whole?" YES → Aggregation.
Q10 — Composition (Strong "Part-of")
A House is composed of Rooms. If the House is demolished, the Rooms cease to exist — they have no meaning without the House. Rooms cannot be moved to another House in this model.
TRICK: Composition = filled diamond ◆ on the OWNER side. Parts CANNOT exist without the whole. Ask: "Can the part exist without the whole?" NO → Composition. Lifecycle is shared.
Q11 — Composition (Order contains OrderItems)
An Order contains one or more OrderItems. If the Order is deleted, all its OrderItems are also deleted — they have no meaning outside an Order.
TRICK: OrderItem is a "part" of Order. You never have a free-floating OrderItem without an Order. This is classic composition. The whole controls the lifecycle of the parts.
Q12 — Aggregation (Department has Employees)
A Department aggregates many Employees. If the Department is closed, the Employees are not deleted — they can be reassigned to another department or become unemployed.
TRICK: HR systems almost always use Aggregation (not Composition) for Department-Employee because employees exist independently. If you fire an employee, the department still exists and vice versa.
Q13 — Composition (Car has Engine)
A Car is composed of an Engine. The Engine exists only as part of this specific Car — it is designed and built for it. If the Car is scrapped, the Engine is scrapped too in this model.
TRICK: In software modelling, Engine is always created inside Car and deleted with it. This differs from real life where you can swap engines — the model depends on the domain rules, not physical reality.
Q14 — Aggregation vs Composition Decision
A Playlist contains many Songs. If the Playlist is deleted, the Songs still exist in the music library. A MusicAlbum however contains Tracks that are deleted when the Album is deleted. Show both.
TRICK: Same physical concept, different rules. Playlist→Song = Aggregation (songs independent). Album→Track = Composition (tracks deleted with album). Always ask: does the PART exist independently?
Q15 — Composition (Form contains Fields)
A RegistrationForm is composed of many FormFields (name field, email field, phone field, etc.). FormFields only exist as part of a specific form — they have no identity outside it.
TRICK: UI components that are created as part of a parent container are almost always Composition. The form "owns" and "creates" all its fields. Without the form, the fields mean nothing.
Q16 — Aggregation (University has Faculties)
A University aggregates many Faculties. Faculties can exist as separate legal entities if the university restructures or merges. A Faculty may also join a different university.
TRICK: University-Faculty is aggregation because faculties can theoretically exist independently (they have their own identity, staff, programs). Compare with Composition where the part has NO independent existence.
Questions 17–24 — Inheritance (Generalization)
Q17 — Inheritance (Simple)
A Person is the superclass. Student and Teacher are subclasses. Both inherit name, age, and contactInfo from Person, but each adds their own specific attributes.
TRICK: Inheritance = hollow triangle arrow FROM child TO parent. Child "IS-A" parent. Child inherits all attributes and methods of parent. Only show EXTRA attributes in child boxes — don't repeat parent attributes.
Q18 — Inheritance (Multi-level)
Animal → Mammal → Dog. Dog inherits from Mammal which inherits from Animal. This is multi-level (3-tier) inheritance. Each level adds more specific attributes.
TRICK: Multi-level inheritance = a chain of arrows going upward. Each subclass inherits from its immediate parent, which in turn inherited from its parent. Don't repeat inherited attributes at lower levels.
Q19 — Inheritance (Multiple Subclasses)
A Vehicle superclass has three subclasses: Car, Truck, and Motorcycle. All three inherit from Vehicle but each has unique attributes (numDoors, cargoCapacity, hasSidecar).
TRICK: Multiple subclasses from one parent = fan of arrows all pointing up to the same parent. Each subclass gets its own box showing ONLY its extra attributes. This is horizontal inheritance breadth.
Q20 — Inheritance (Abstract Class)
A Shape abstract class cannot be instantiated directly. Circle and Rectangle are concrete subclasses. Shape defines an abstract method calculateArea() that both subclasses must override.
TRICK: Abstract class = written in italics or with «abstract» stereotype. Abstract methods in italics inside the class box. Subclasses must implement all abstract methods or be abstract themselves.
Q21 — Inheritance (Payment types)
Abstract Payment class is the parent. CashPayment, VisaPayment, and OnlineTransfer are concrete children. Each implements processPayment() differently.
TRICK: Payment type hierarchy is a classic exam scenario. All children share: paymentID, amount, date. Each adds unique fields. processPayment() is overridden (polymorphism). Very common in all system designs.
Q22 — Inheritance (User types)
Abstract User has subclasses: Admin, Member, and Guest. All share login() and logout(). Admin adds manageSystem(), Member adds viewContent(), Guest can only browsePublic().
TRICK: User hierarchy is the most common class diagram in exams. Always: abstract parent → concrete children with IS-A relationship. Check: "Is Admin a User?" YES → inheritance correct.
Q23 — Inheritance (Room types)
Abstract Room class has subclasses: SingleRoom, DoubleRoom, and Suite. All share roomID, pricePerNight, and checkAvailability(). Each adds unique features.
TRICK: Domain specialization — different room types share base behavior (pricing, availability) but differ in amenities. Show only the EXTRA attributes unique to each subclass inside the child boxes.
Q24 — Inheritance (Employee roles)
Abstract Employee is the superclass. FullTimeEmployee and PartTimeEmployee inherit from it. FullTime has annualSalary, PartTime has hourlyRate and hoursWorked.
TRICK: Employment type hierarchy. calculatePay() is overridden differently: FullTime uses annualSalary/12, PartTime uses hourlyRate × hoursWorked. This is the power of polymorphism through inheritance.
Questions 25–32 — Dependency & Realization
Q25 — Dependency
A ReportGenerator uses a DatabaseConnection temporarily to fetch data and produce a report. It does not store a permanent reference to the connection — it just uses it locally during execution.
TRICK: Dependency = dashed arrow ---→. The WEAKEST relationship. A class uses another temporarily (method parameter, local variable, return type). NO permanent attribute reference. "Uses temporarily" = Dependency.
Q26 — Realization (Interface Implementation)
Printable is an interface with method print(). Invoice and Receipt both realize (implement) the Printable interface. They must each provide their own implementation of print().
TRICK: Realization = dashed arrow with HOLLOW triangle ---▷. Used when a class implements an interface. Interface name written in «guillemets». All interface methods must be implemented by the realizing class.
Q27 — Dependency (Class uses Utility)
A UserService class depends on an EmailSender utility class. When a user registers, UserService calls EmailSender.send() — but does not hold a permanent reference to it.
TRICK: Service classes depending on utility/helper classes = Dependency. The utility is passed as a parameter or called statically. No attribute field stored. Compare: if UserService had "private EmailSender sender" = that would be Association.
Q28 — Realization (Comparable Interface)
Sortable is an interface requiring compareTo() and sort() methods. ProductList realizes this interface so that products can be sorted by price or name in the shopping system.
TRICK: Any class that "implements" an interface uses Realization. In code: class ProductList implements Sortable. In UML: dashed hollow triangle arrow FROM ProductList TO Sortable interface.
Q29 — Dependency (Factory Pattern)
A NotificationFactory creates different notification objects. It depends on SMSNotification and EmailNotification classes — they are created and returned by the factory but not stored as fields.
TRICK: Factory classes depend on the classes they create (return type dependency). The factory doesn't hold a permanent reference — it creates on demand. This is the most common Dependency pattern in OOP design.
Q30 — Realization (Multiple interfaces)
A SmartDevice class realizes two interfaces: Connectable (for WiFi/Bluetooth) and Updatable (for software updates). A class can implement multiple interfaces in Java/OOP.
TRICK: One class can realize MULTIPLE interfaces — just draw separate dashed hollow triangle arrows to each interface. This is how OOP achieves "multiple interface inheritance" even without multiple class inheritance.
Q31 — Dependency vs Association distinction
Class A has a private field of type B → Association. Class C receives D as a method parameter → Dependency. Show both patterns side by side to understand the difference clearly.
TRICK: The KEY difference — Association = stored as a field (permanent link). Dependency = only in method signature (temporary). If you can see it as an attribute in the class body → Association. If only in method → Dependency.
Q32 — Realization (Repository Pattern)
UserRepository interface defines database operations. MySQLUserRepository and MongoUserRepository each realize this interface with their own implementations.
TRICK: Repository pattern = interface (what to do) + multiple concrete implementations (how to do it). Each concrete class realizes the interface differently. Allows swapping database without changing business logic.
Questions 33–40 — Mixed Relationships (Full System Diagrams)
Q33 — Full System: ATM (mixed)
ATM System: Customer has-a BankAccount (Association). Transaction is composed-of Receipt (Composition). WithdrawTransaction inherits from Transaction (Inheritance). Customer depends-on ATM (Dependency).
TRICK: Map each sentence to a relationship type. "has-a" → Association. "composed-of" (receipt only exists in transaction) → Composition. "type-of" → Inheritance. "uses temporarily" → Dependency.
Q34 — Full System: Library (mixed)
Library: Member associates with Book (borrows). BorrowRecord composed of Fine. Librarian inherits from Staff. Admin inherits from Staff. Staff depends-on LibrarySystem.
TRICK: When multiple relationships exist — draw systematically. Start with inheritance (top), then composition (strong), then association (medium), then dependency (weak). Work top-down in relationship strength.
Q35 — Full System: Hospital (mixed)
Doctor (inherits from MedicalStaff) treats many Patients (Association). Each Patient has a composed MedicalRecord (Composition). Appointment depends on Schedule (Dependency).
TRICK: MedicalRecord is composition because it cannot exist without the Patient — it IS the patient's data. Doctor-Patient is association because both exist independently (Doctor exists even with no patients, and patient data persists).
Q36 — All 4 main relationships in one diagram
Show all four relationships together: Company aggregates Departments. Department composes Projects. Manager inherits from Employee. Manager depends on ReportTemplate.
TRICK: When you see all 4 in one diagram — strength order: Composition strongest → Aggregation → Association → Dependency weakest. Inheritance is a separate category (is-a, not has-a). Use different line styles to distinguish clearly.
Q37 — Full System: E-Commerce (mixed)
Customer places Orders (1..*). Order contains OrderItems (composition). Product is stocked by Seller. VisaPayment and CashPayment inherit from abstract Payment. Order depends on PaymentGateway.
TRICK: E-Commerce is THE most common full system diagram exam question. Memorize this structure: Customer→Order (assoc), Order→OrderItem (comp), Payment→children (inh), Order→Gateway (dep).
Q38 — Identifying wrong relationships (Pitfall)
A student wrote: "Window has-a Title" as Composition. "Library owns Books" as Dependency. "Dog is-a Animal" as Association. Identify all 3 ERRORS and show the correct relationships.
TRICK: Wrong → Correct: 1) Window-Title = Composition ✓ (title destroyed with window). 2) Library-Book = Aggregation ✓ not Dependency (library stores books permanently). 3) Dog-Animal = Inheritance ✓ (IS-A, not association).
Q39 — Full System: Course Registration (mixed)
Student registers for Courses through CourseRegistration (association class). Registration has composed Bill. Instructor and Student both inherit from Person. Bill depends on PaymentSystem.
TRICK: Association class (CourseRegistration) appears when a many-to-many relationship has its own attributes (semester, status, grade). It becomes a class sitting on the association line with a dashed connector.
Q40 — Relationships Quick Reference + Final Challenge
The Hotel System final challenge: Guest (inherits Person) makes Reservations. Reservation composed-of Bill. Reservation associates Room. Receptionist (inherits Person) manages Room. Bill realizes Payable interface. Bill depends on PaymentGateway.
TRICK: Final exam approach: (1) Find all classes. (2) For each pair: ask IS-A? HAS-A strong? HAS-A weak? USES-TEMP? IMPLEMENTS? → assign relationship. Draw inheritance tree first, then add other relationships.
